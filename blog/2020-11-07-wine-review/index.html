Full review

This isn't something you see while wine tasting. This was only discovered when I inquired more about the different woods used and how they are prepared before being used in the winery. In a nutshell, most wines use one type of wood, but possibly different vessels along the way. Del Dotti chose to use two different types of woods on the stave of a barrel. Traditionally, one would imagine using one type of wood but Del Dotti chose to use two. One being American and one being French.

This leads to difference choices in how to create the wine. The important thing to remember is that the wood flavor is a lot strong and more unique. Thus the wine (grapes) itself need to be balanced rather than over-powering and indeed that is the case. The tannin isn't as strong and the wood feels like the main piece with the grapes being more of the background. Usually once would except more of the flavor from the grapes but it's well balanced with the wood.

The wood takes center stage and defines the wine here. The start is a strong, sharp, bold American wood flavor that leads into the quieter and sweeter grape profile. The tail end is the more typical French wood oak that's apparent in the end. It's a very long and noticable wood flavor that carries as usual. What is unique is that it embodies these flavors without overpowering each other. This is no different than listening to a band rather than a solo. Each has it's own merits but in this case, the group is more interesting than the individual flavor. It is possible to taste the single-oak only variants but it is not as unique or interesting.

The price is not cheap, rather it's the price of the two other single-oak varieities together. It's about five hundred USD rather than about two fifty for each. Of course, there is half the quantity, rather than buying both and combining them but alas it probably won't be the same. However, there is a price for an unique experiment and the experience that comes with it and in this case it's worth it.
